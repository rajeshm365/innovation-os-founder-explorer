PayrollShield

Does the pre-run payroll reconciliation as a completed service — every change classified, every retro-calculation computed, delivered ready to approve.

01

Opportunity snapshot

Category: Payroll Accuracy Operations. Pattern: AI-Native Service Company — Tier S in our preference criteria: "Don't build a tool that helps people do accounting. Just do the accounting."

Why it survived previous stages

PayrollShield achieved the strongest cross-AI consensus of any company in FPA-12. Claude identified strong venture potential. Gemini gave it a GO — "the foundational wedge for the entire problem space," requiring zero new domain knowledge, with an MVP deliverable manually in 2–6 weeks. Perplexity independently selected it as build-first: the buyer is reachable, the pain is externally corroborated by Indian payroll-compliance evidence, and the validation loop runs weekly rather than quarterly.

Of all ten generated companies, the artifact rated it highest on founder-market fit and validation speed.

02

Understand the world

Forget startups for ten minutes. Let me show you what actually happens in the 48 hours before payroll runs at a big Indian company. Tap through — the company doesn't appear until the pain is obvious.

There is a person — let's call her the payroll administrator — sitting inside a large Indian enterprise. Think of an IT services company with more than 10,000 employees. Her job sounds simple: make sure every employee gets paid the correct amount, on time, every month.

↓

The company runs two separate systems. The first is the HR system (an HRIS) — where HR records everything that happens to an employee: someone joins, resigns, gets promoted, gets a salary revision, transfers cities.

The second is the payroll system — the engine that actually computes salaries. In India, this is very often run by an outsourced payroll provider, a separate company hired to process salaries.

They're connected by a batch sync: payroll doesn't learn about HR changes instantly. It receives them later, in a scheduled bundle — a delta file, simply a list of "here is everything that changed since the last update." This is not a bug. The payroll engines are built this way, by design, for over ten years in companies running SAP or Oracle HR with ADP-style payroll.

Her month: in the 48 hours before final payroll runs, she opens that delta file and reads every single change, asking one question for each: "Does this affect someone's pay — and how?"
↓

HR records a termination — but it is backdated. The employee actually left three months ago; the paperwork arrived late. This single entry quietly triggers a chain:

  • PF (Provident Fund — the retirement contribution every employer must deposit by law) has been wrongly deducted for three months and must be recalculated backwards. In the evidence, one such change involved ₹47,000 of retro-calculation.
  • ESIC (the state employee insurance scheme) registration must be reversed retroactively.
  • TDS (Tax Deducted at Source — the income tax cut from salary) must be recomputed.

That word — retro-calculation — just means redoing past months' calculations because something changed after the fact. India produces a lot of these: backdated exits, statutory thresholds, state-wise rules, and now the new Labour Codes changing the rules while the game is being played.

↓

One payroll cycle contains hundreds of changes. Any one of them could hide a chain like the one above. She must catch every single one — manually.

↓

Home-grown Excel workbooks full of macros. Personal checklists. Memory. She cross-references the delta file against the payroll cycle by hand and builds an override package — corrections she sends to the payroll system before the run.

↓
  • Excel and memory do not scale with volume.
  • The knowledge lives in one person's head.
  • The statutory rules keep moving — the Labour Codes are changing the rules mid-game.
  • The outsourced provider adds yet another layer where things slip.
↓

A wrong amount reaches an employee's paycheck. The company must run an off-cycle correction run — an emergency extra payroll cycle costing $5,000–$15,000 each time. EPFO (the Provident Fund authority) can issue penalty notices. Unions raise grievances. Legal gets involved. Employees stop trusting their payslips.

The opening

Here is the part that matters most. The administrator feels the labor — but she does not feel the financial pain. The person who does is the Controller or VP Finance — the finance leader who answers for the correction run, the penalty notice, the audit finding.

The HR vendor hands over a raw file. The payroll vendor runs an engine. The dangerous intelligence work in between — "which of these changes will blow up?" — belongs to nobody. It is an orphan, done by hand, every cycle, forever. Ab samajh aaya? Good — now let's see what we'd build.

03

What would we build?

PayrollShield does the pre-run reconciliation as a completed service. It sits exactly in the orphaned gap — the delta file still arrives, the batch sync still exists, but the dangerous manual reading of it disappears. No technology jargon, just the flow.

Inputs

The enterprise shares two things it already has: the HR delta file and the payroll cycle data.

↓
What happens

PayrollShield returns a finished exception package: every change classified, every retro-calculation already computed, every override pre-formatted — before the payroll run.

↓
Where AI helps

The detective work: classifying which of the hundreds of changes are dangerous, mapping cross-system consequences ("this backdated exit triggers PF retro + ESIC reversal + TDS recompute"), and computing the multi-step retro-calculations that today live in her Excel macros.

↓
Where humans stay involved

The administrator reviews and clicks "approve" — she becomes a reviewer, not a producer. Human sign-off stays by design (the evidence is explicit that compliance requires it). On our side: the first cycles are delivered manually with AI augmentation — Rajesh reviews the delta file, Rahul's pipeline classifies and computes, and Ranjan verifies every output against official sources (EPFO circulars, ESIC notifications, Income Tax Act sections) before anything reaches the client.

↓
Outputs

A ready-to-approve exception package, every cycle. ₹50K–₹1L per cycle for the first client. Revenue from week 3, productizing after clients 3–5.

Why a company, not a feature

It owns the reconciliation workflow end to end (workflow grade C — the work disappears from the client). The incumbents built delta files — raw data. None built the intelligence on top, because the admin's manual labor is invisible to their contracts. And it is the validated starting motion toward the locked secondary paths: ProviderOS and AuditOps run on the same engine.

04

Why might customers care?

The pain today

Every cycle, hundreds of changes read manually in a 48-hour window, any one of which can hide a retro-calculation chain. The admin works in fear of the one change she missed.

What it costs

Off-cycle correction runs at $5,000–$15,000 each. EPFO penalty notices. Union grievances. Labour Code audit exposure. Employee trust erosion.

Why today's coping fails

Excel and memory don't scale; the knowledge lives in one head; statutory rules keep moving (Labour Code rollout); and the outsourced provider adds another layer where things slip.

Why they might pay

The Controller buys risk elimination — no payroll error reaching a paycheck on their watch — not software. One prevented correction run pays for months of the service. On per-cycle pricing acceptance specifically: Insufficient evidence available from previous stages.

ProviderOS

The AI verification engine inside India's outsourced payroll providers — letting them offer accuracy guarantees their competitors cannot match.

01

Opportunity snapshot

Category: Payroll Provider Intelligence Infrastructure. Pattern: Vertical AI Software — owning the verification-and-delivery workflow inside the provider.

Why it survived — and why it's secondary, not first

The generation artifact itself named ProviderOS its single company to build, and Gemini agreed with a GO — "the smartest GTM execution of the FPA-12 artifact": it bypasses brutal enterprise procurement by selling to owner-operated provider firms, and one provider deal opens 10–30 enterprise payroll datasets at once.

But Perplexity held it at MONITOR — the provider-adoption assumption is plausible but not externally validated, and the channel could slow early learning if providers treat verification as a burden rather than a sales enabler. The locked consensus: PayrollShield first (it validates the foundational buyer), ProviderOS as the credible expansion path — the same engine, sold into the provider channel once the pain is proven.

02

Understand the world

Same world as PayrollShield — but step inside the outsourced provider's office this time. (If you haven't read the payroll administrator's story yet, start with PayrollShield.)

India's outsourced payroll providers — the firms enterprises hire to actually run salaries — employ 10–30 statutory specialists who manually compute PF, ESIC, TDS, professional tax, and gratuity for every mid-cycle change. Their technology stack, per the evidence: Excel and institutional memory.

Quality depends on individual knowledge. When a specialist leaves, their edge-case knowledge leaves with them. And the Labour Code rollout changes the rules faster than manual teams can retrain.

Here's the twist: providers profit from manual billable hours — they have no incentive to automate themselves. ProviderOS reframes it: not "automation" (revenue destruction) but a competitive weapon — the provider who adopts can tell enterprise clients "every statutory calculation is independently AI-verified before delivery." Competitors cannot make that claim.
03

What would we build?

Inputs

The provider's statutory calculations and the underlying HR change data for each enterprise client, inside the provider's existing delivery workflow.

↓
Where AI helps

Independently verifying every statutory calculation — PF contributions, ESIC applicability, TDS computation, retro-calculations — before the provider delivers output to the enterprise client.

↓
Where humans stay involved

The provider's specialists become an approval layer reviewing AI-verified output, instead of computing everything by hand. On our side, Ranjan's verification methodology — checking against EPFO circulars, ESIC notifications, Income Tax Act sections — IS the product's quality layer.

↓
Outputs

A contractual claim competitors can't make: "Every statutory calculation is independently AI-verified before delivery." Pricing: ₹10–20/employee/month through the provider — for a provider serving 20K total employees, ₹2–4L/month.

04

Why might customers care?

The pain today

Manual statutory computation, person-dependent quality, knowledge that walks out the door, and Labour Code changes outpacing retraining.

The market pressure

Larger enterprises increasingly demand SLA-backed accuracy guarantees that manual providers cannot credibly offer.

Why they might pay

Client acquisition advantage, not cost reduction. When an enterprise evaluates three providers, the one offering AI-verified accuracy with a contractual guarantee wins. The provider's ROI is measured in enterprise clients won.

01

Opportunity snapshot

Category: Payroll Control Assurance Operations. Pattern: AI-Native Service Company — not a tool for auditors, but the audit itself, delivered as a finished workpaper.

Why it survived — and why it's secondary, not first

Gemini gave AuditOps a GO: "a robust, highly targeted application of the FPA-12 core engine that taps into mandatory compliance budgets rather than discretionary ops budgets" — and an excellent use of Ranjan's QA background translated into SOX-grade audit work.

02

Understand the world

Same world — but now meet a different team in the same building: internal audit.

Internal audit teams at large enterprises are required to test payroll accuracy — for US-listed Indian companies (think Infosys, TCS, Wipro), it's part of SOX compliance; on top of that sit Indian statutory audit requirements (EPFO, ESIC).

How do they do it today? Manual sampling: select 50–100 employees per quarter from a population of 10,000+, recalculate their payroll by hand, check HRIS alignment, document findings. 2–3 weeks of analyst time per cycle.

The sample covers less than 1% of the population. Sampling is accepted — but known to miss errors. Full-population testing was always better; it was simply too expensive to do by hand. Per the evidence, that cost inversion flipped: AI-powered full-population analysis is now cheaper than human sample-based testing.
03

After each payroll cycle: the payroll register and the HRIS export.

↓

The full-population comparison — every employee, every calculation, every statutory amount — the work that made 100% coverage impossible by hand.

↓
Where humans stay involved

The internal audit team reviews the delivered workpaper and files it — the audit stays theirs to sign. On our side, Ranjan's verification methodology — rigorous, documented, traceable — is what makes the output audit-grade.

↓
Outputs

A completed audit workpaper with findings classified by severity, root cause, and remediation recommendation. ₹2–5L per audit cycle, delivered quarterly — ₹8–20L per client per year.

2–3 weeks of analyst time per audit cycle for under 1% coverage — a methodology everyone accepts and everyone knows misses errors.

Indian enterprises carry both SOX obligations (US-listed entities) and Indian statutory audit requirements (EPFO, ESIC) — a double compliance load that raises audit demand.

Why they might pay

The Chief Audit Executive gets 100% coverage at sample-testing cost, with analysts freed for higher-value audit work — paid from mandatory compliance budgets, not discretionary ops budgets.
