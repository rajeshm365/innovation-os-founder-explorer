ShipRight

A checkpoint with a financial guarantee. It verifies every order before it ships — and if it clears an order that turns out wrong, it pays.

01

Opportunity snapshot

Category: Pre-Fulfillment Order Assurance. Pattern: AI-Native Service Company — the customer pays for an outcome (zero mis-shipments), not for software.

Why it survived previous stages

Three AI committees looked at ten companies. Claude originally favored ResolveAI's upside. Gemini elevated ShipRight for founder fit and the lowest adoption friction in the whole set — read-only ERP access, zero workflow disruption, shadow-mode proof. Perplexity confirmed the verification problem is commercially real and that ShipRight has the fastest validation loop of all candidates.

The consensus judged ShipRight the strongest allocation of founder time as a first move: a trust-building wedge that can later open the door to the bigger automation plays.

02

Understand the world

Forget startups for ten minutes. Tap through the story below — one step at a time. The company doesn't appear until the pain is obvious.

Picture one person. Call her Meena — the name doesn't matter, the work does. She sits at the order desk of a mid-sized manufacturing company. Not a giant like Tata, not a tiny shop — a company doing $50M to $500M in revenue, making things like auto parts or industrial components, selling to other businesses.

Her job sounds simple: when a customer sends an order, make sure the company's computer system has it recorded correctly, so the warehouse ships exactly the right goods.

↓

Customers don't order through some neat standard form. They send a PO — a purchase order. It's just a document saying "we want to buy these items, this many, deliver here." Usually a PDF attached to an email. In India, sometimes just a WhatsApp message.

Every customer's PO looks different. Different layout, different product names, different shorthand.

Meena reads the PO, figures out what the customer actually wants, and types it into the ERP — the central software where the company records orders, stock, and billing. In Indian mid-market companies, that's usually Tally or SAP Business One.

Once the order is in the ERP, the warehouse takes over. The warehouse never reads the original PO. The warehouse trusts whatever is in the ERP.
↓

A PO arrives saying: "5 boxes of BW-1."

Open the company's product catalog — there is no "BW-1." That's the customer's internal code, not the seller's. Meena knows from past orders that BW-1 means "Blue Widget Mark I."

Now, "5 boxes." A box of what? For this particular customer, Meena knows "box" means a case of 12 — because that's how this customer has ordered for their last 40 orders. So she enters 60 units into the ERP.

None of this was written on the PO. She filled the gaps from memory and experience. It takes about 12 minutes per order. A team handling 1,500 orders a month spends roughly 300 hours a month doing exactly this.
↓

Somewhere between 1 and 3 out of every 100 orders gets entered wrong. A wrong quantity. A wrong product. A "box" read as 1 unit instead of 12.

And here's the dangerous part: nothing checks her work. The ERP only checks basics — quantity is not zero, the product code exists. It cannot ask, "is this right for this customer?" The warehouse picks exactly what the system says. The wrong goods get packed, loaded, and shipped.

The error is discovered by the customer — when the wrong shipment lands at their door.
↓

Companies cope with experience. The senior person "just knows" each customer's quirks. Some keep personal Excel files and notes. When time allows, someone double-checks the entry against the PO.

↓
  • The knowledge lives in heads, not systems. It's written nowhere — not in the ERP, not in any official document. This role sees 25–40% annual turnover. When the experienced person quits, the knowledge walks out the door, and the replacement makes errors for months.
  • Double-checking depends on free time. Busy season is exactly when errors spike — and exactly when nobody has time to verify.
  • There is no checkpoint in the process itself. Between "order entered in the ERP" and "warehouse starts picking," no systematic verification step exists. Not in the ERP. Not in the warehouse system. Nowhere.
↓

When the wrong shipment arrives, the seller pays: return freight, replacement shipping, credit notes, angry phone calls. A single wrong shipment costs $200 to $2,000 to fix. Across a month, companies lose $10,000 to $50,000 to these errors.

For key accounts, it's worse. Large buyers often have an SLA — a written promise of accuracy and timeliness, with money penalties if you break it. Repeated wrong shipments don't just cost money — they put the entire account at risk of leaving.

And disputes are ugly: the buyer says "I never ordered that," the seller says "but that's what you wrote," and nobody can prove who was right.

The opening

Notice the gap. The error happens at the order desk. The error becomes expensive at the customer's door. Between those two points — after the entry, before the warehouse picks — sits a window where the error exists but hasn't cost anything yet.

Nobody in this world owns that window. Ab samajh aaya? Good — now we can talk about what we'd build.

03

What would we build?

ShipRight is a checkpoint with a financial guarantee. It sits in that exact window — between "order entered" and "warehouse picks" — and verifies every order before it ships. Here's the flow, no technology jargon.

Inputs

Three things, all read-only: the order as entered in the ERP, the original PO the buyer sent, and that buyer's order history.

Nothing in the customer's workflow changes. CSRs keep their jobs. ShipRight watches and verifies — it doesn't replace anyone and never writes into the ERP.

↓
What happens

For every order, before the warehouse touches it, ShipRight compares the ERP entry against the original PO and the customer's history. Does "5 boxes" entered as 60 units match how this buyer always orders? Does the product match what the PO actually named?

↓
Where AI helps

The cross-reading. AI can read the ERP entry, the original PO, and the order history together in one pass, and learn each customer's patterns — like "this buyer's box means a case of 12." Older rule-based checks could only catch basics (quantity not zero, valid product code); they could never catch business-logic errors.

↓
Where humans stay involved

When ShipRight flags an order as suspicious, a human looks at it — the customer's people stay in the loop, and our team handles exception escalation. And before any guarantee is switched on, the team runs shadow mode: verifying real orders in parallel with the existing process and counting the errors caught, with human comparison of every result. That's our QA discipline applied to the product.

↓
Outputs

Cleared orders flow to the warehouse. Suspicious orders are flagged before shipping — while the error is still free to fix.

The guarantee — what makes this a company, not a feature

If ShipRight clears an order and it ships wrong anyway, ShipRight pays the re-shipping and credit costs. The customer is not buying software. They are buying zero mis-shipments, with financial backing. Detection alone is a feature; paying when you're wrong is a business.

04

Why might customers care?

The pain today

1–3 of every 100 orders is entered wrong, and nothing in the process checks the work. The error is discovered by the customer, after delivery.

What it costs

$200–$2,000 per incident to fix. $10,000–$50,000 lost per month. SLA penalties on key accounts. Repeated errors put whole accounts at risk of leaving — and disputes nobody can prove either way.

Why today's coping fails

The knowledge lives in heads (and this role turns over 25–40% a year). Double-checking depends on free time, which vanishes exactly when errors spike. And no checkpoint exists anywhere in the stack between ERP entry and warehouse picking.

Why they might pay

Risk transfer. Mis-shipments cost $10,000–$50,000 a month; ShipRight charges ₹75,000–1,50,000/month (or ₹50–150 per order) and takes on the risk. The pitch isn't "better software" — it's "your error cost becomes our problem."

ResolveAI

Replaces the order operations department: orders arrive in any format, ERP-ready records come out, billed per order processed correctly.

01

Opportunity snapshot

Category: Order Resolution Operations. Pattern: AI-Native Service Company — the customer pays for completed work, not a tool.

Why it survived — and why it's secondary, not first

Claude originally rated this the highest-upside opportunity in the set: the keystone. Once ResolveAI operates, it accumulates the customer rules, deployment patterns, and resolution data that every other company in the portfolio is built from.

But the consensus placed it second in sequence. Gemini judged the trust barrier heavier — it asks a manufacturer to replace headcount and let an AI write into their ERP. Perplexity rated the surrounding market crowded and held it at MONITOR. The locked sequence: ShipRight earns trust first; ResolveAI is the natural upsell behind it.

02

Understand the world

Same world as ShipRight — Meena at the order desk. (If you haven't read her story yet, start with ShipRight's "Understand the world.") The difference is which part of her day this company attacks.

Remember the numbers: about 12 minutes per order of reading, interpreting, looking things up, and re-keying. 300 hours a month for a team handling 1,500 orders. 40–60% of non-EDI POs fail straight-through processing — not because the text is hard to read, but because of business logic: customer product codes, unit conversions, unwritten rules.

That entire interpretation job — "BW-1 means Blue Widget Mark I, and this buyer's box means 12" — is exactly what ResolveAI's agents would do. Orders in any format go in; ERP-ready records come out; a human reviews only the roughly 5% the agents cannot resolve.

The customer stops employing people for this function. That's the promise — and also the problem, as we'll see.
03

What would we build?

Inputs

The buyer's PO in any format — PDF email, WhatsApp message — plus the customer's history and product catalog.

↓
Where AI helps

The full resolution chain: identify the customer, map their product codes to ours, convert units ("box" → case of 12), apply their shipping preferences and unwritten rules, and assemble an ERP-ready record.

↓
Where humans stay involved

A human reviews only the ~5% of orders the agents cannot resolve, plus exception escalation. And every first deployment runs in shadow mode — AI working in parallel with the CSRs while the team compares accuracy for at least two weeks.

↓
Outputs

Resolved, ERP-ready order records — billed per order processed correctly (₹100–250/order in India). The customer buys completed work, not software.

Why might customers care?

The pain today

300 hours/month of resolution work per 1,500-order team. 20–40% of a sales rep's workweek consumed. 1–3% error rates feeding mis-shipments and SLA penalties.

Why coping fails

The work depends on tribal knowledge in CSR heads, with 25–40% annual turnover. When the experienced person quits, the knowledge evaporates and errors spike for months.

Headcount economics. 1,000 orders/month at ₹200/order is ₹2L/month — versus 3 CSRs at ₹1.5–2.4L/month plus training, benefits, turnover cost, and knowledge-loss risk. ResolveAI's cost is variable, scales with volume, and carries zero turnover risk.

TradeLogic

The system of record for the unwritten business rules in every buyer–seller relationship — knowledge that today lives only in people's heads.

01

Opportunity snapshot

Category: Buyer-Seller Business Logic Infrastructure. Pattern: Company Brain — capturing the knowledge trapped in heads, files, and email history.

Why it survived — and why it's secondary, not first

TradeLogic has the highest defensibility in the whole set: accumulated rules across hundreds of buyer–seller relationships cannot be replicated without equivalent deployment history. Both committees see it as a credible long-term asset.

But it's the wrong cold-start. Gemini judged the trust barrier of asking companies to hand over years of historical PO and ERP data too high for an unknown part-time team. The locked view: TradeLogic becomes natural once a core system (ShipRight, then ResolveAI) is operating and generating that data as a side effect.

02

Understand the world

Same world again — but this time, look at what happens when Meena leaves.

Everything Meena knows — that BW-1 means Blue Widget Mark I, that this buyer's "box" means 12, who likes which courier, which substitute part is acceptable — exists in exactly one place: her head. Maybe a personal Excel file. Maybe sticky notes.

No enterprise system stores it. The CRM stores contacts. The ERP stores product masters. Nothing stores "how Customer X orders from us and what they mean."

This role sees 25–40% annual turnover. When Meena quits, five years of accumulated knowledge evaporates, and her replacement makes elevated errors for 3–6 months while re-learning it all. Then the cycle repeats.

TradeLogic's bet: an AI can read 500 past orders side-by-side with what was entered in the ERP, and infer the rules — "when this customer says box, the CSR entered case of 12" — then store them permanently and serve them to any new person or AI system that needs them.

03

What would we build?

Inputs

Historical pairs: the original POs a buyer sent, next to what was actually entered in the ERP for each one.

↓
Where AI helps

Inferring the rules from those pairs — product code mappings, unit conventions, shipping preferences, substitution paths — without anyone having to write them down.

↓
Where humans stay involved

Validating the inferred rules against historical data with QA rigor before anything trusts them, and managing the data ingestion with each customer.

↓
Outputs

A permanent, validated rulebook per buyer relationship — served to whoever needs it next: a new hire, or an AI agent. When the experienced person quits, nothing is lost. Subscription: ₹25,000–75,000/month based on buyer relationships managed.

04

Why might customers care?

The pain today

Customer-specific rules live in CSR heads, personal Excel files, sticky notes, and informal conversations — recorded in no enterprise system.

The consequence

A CSR quits → 3–6 months of elevated errors while the replacement re-learns → the cycle repeats. The trigger moment: "Our best CSR quit. Her replacement makes 3x the errors. We need her knowledge in a system."

Why they might pay

Institutional memory insurance. The person who "just knows" is a single point of failure — and PE ownership of mid-market companies makes undocumented business rules a diligence red flag.
