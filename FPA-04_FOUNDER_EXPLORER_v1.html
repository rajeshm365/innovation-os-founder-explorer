Build-First · Locked

ReconForce

A managed service that investigates and resolves reconciliation exceptions — the customer forwards an email, a completed investigation brief comes back.

01

Opportunity snapshot

Category: Exception Investigation Operations. Pattern: AI-Native Service Company — Tier S in our preference criteria: "Don't build a tool that helps people do accounting. Just do the accounting." Outcome-based, per-result pricing.

Why it survived previous stages

Both independent validators — Gemini and Perplexity — returned GO verdicts, agreeing with the original recommendation. Three reasons kept repeating across all three committees:

Zero integration: forwarding an email is the entire onboarding — no APIs, no IT projects, no software deployment. The fastest possible kill test: the one assumption everything depends on — will analysts trust an AI brief enough to approve it — can be tested in a single week with real exceptions and no product built. The validation vehicle for the entire FPA-04 surface: if AI cannot investigate at trusted quality, no company in this domain works, and ReconForce reveals that in days rather than months.

Even its worst case leaves behind a resolution pattern database and real BPO relationships. Gemini called it "the ideal use of founder time."

02

Understand the world

Forget startups for ten minutes. Let me walk you through one person's working day. If this part makes sense, everything after it is easy. Tap through — the company doesn't appear until the pain is obvious.

There is a job called reconciliation analyst. The word sounds heavy, so here's what it actually means. Every large company runs on many computer systems that were bought separately and never properly connected. Money-related information sits in more than one system at a time — a payment shows up in one system, and a record of that same payment shows up in another.

Reconciliation simply means checking that the two systems agree about the same money. When they don't agree, that mismatch is called an exception — and a human being has to find out why.

Thousands of these analysts sit in Bangalore, Hyderabad, Pune and Chennai inside BPO centers — firms like TCS, Infosys, Wipro or Genpact that banks hire to run their back-office work. There are also permanent in-house versions of this job at Amazon, JPMorgan and Fidelity — confirmed during validation through current job postings at Citi, Deutsche Bank and Bank of America. This is not a dying job. It is a permanent, hiring-right-now job.

↓

When two systems disagree about money, someone must figure out why, fix it, and write down how they fixed it — before deadlines pass and before inspectors come asking.

The first pass is done by software. Matching tools like BlackLine automatically match 84–99% of records — the easy cases where everything lines up. What's left after the daily run is the analyst's queue: roughly 30 exceptions land on their desk each day. Each one takes 30–45 minutes to investigate.

↓

An exception lands: a transaction exists in System A but won't match in System B. The analyst opens old email threads. Reads the contract terms with the other company involved — the counterparty, meaning the business on the other side of the transaction. Checks how similar cases were resolved before. Still unclear, so they email the counterparty and wait — replies take one to five days.

The answer finally comes back: the counterparty migrated to a new system, and the new system writes timestamps in a different format. The fix is reformatting one field. The analyst classifies the root cause, documents every step for the auditors, closes the exception.

Forty-five minutes of work, spread across days of waiting, for one mismatch.
↓

Thirty exceptions a day, at 30–45 minutes each, is more than a full working day. The queue never empties.

On top of that, 15–25% of the analyst's time goes into the email loop alone — composing inquiries, chasing replies, reading attachments.

And the most valuable thing in the room — the knowledge of how to resolve each pattern — lives only inside the heads of experienced analysts. "I've seen this 200 times" is the actual operating system of these teams.

↓
  • Companies buy better matching software — already done, and it worked: matching is solved.
  • Companies hire more cheap labor — a BPO analyst costs about $15 an hour, so adding people is the standard answer.
  • Teams lean on their veterans' memory.
↓

The software can't help, because what remains is not matching — it's reading emails, understanding contracts, and making judgment calls. One validated data point says it all: an organization with 96% automatic matching reduced headcount by only two people. The machines took the easy 60–70%; the human 30–40% stayed fully human.

Hiring can't keep up either — exception volumes grow faster than teams can recruit and train. And the veterans leave: BPO teams lose 20–30% of their people every year, and when a senior analyst walks out, years of pattern knowledge walks out with them, while a replacement takes months to ramp.

Finally, the humans can't simply be removed even in theory — regulations often require that a second person independently checks decisions, and every resolution must be documented for audit. The human role is deliberate risk control, not laziness.

↓

Exceptions age past the deadlines the bank has promised — the SLAs, service level agreements. Auditors find undocumented resolutions and issue findings, which trigger emergency budget and panic. A senior departure creates overnight risk. Costs rise every year with no ceiling.

The opening

So that's the world. The easy majority of this work is already automated. What remains — investigating, judging, chasing counterparties, documenting — is done by hand, at enormous scale, by people whose knowledge evaporates when they resign.

That leftover slice is the opportunity. Ab samajh aaya? Good — now the company.

03

What would we build?

ReconForce is a managed service that does the investigation itself. The investigation work exits the customer's organization. No technology jargon — just the flow.

Inputs

The customer's analyst forwards an exception — literally forwards an email. That is the entire onboarding. No system connections, no software installed, no IT project.

↓
What happens

On ReconForce's side, the work the analyst would have done gets done: read the related emails, check the contract terms, compare against prior resolutions, classify the root cause. Back comes a completed investigation brief showing the evidence and the recommended resolution — within 24 hours.

↓
Where AI helps

The reading and cross-referencing — emails, contract terms, prior resolution patterns — that consumes the analyst's 30–45 minutes per exception. Every investigated exception also feeds a resolution pattern database, so the next investigation gets better.

↓
Where humans stay involved

On the customer's side: their analyst reviews the brief in 3–5 minutes, approves, and closes — the regulatory requirement for human checking stays intact; the analyst becomes an approver instead of an investigator. On our side: AI plus a domain specialist do the investigation, and every brief passes verification rigor before it's sent — accuracy at regulatory standards is the product.

↓
Outputs

Resolved exceptions, priced per exception: ₹200–500 each. The customer buys outcomes, not software seats.

Why a company, not a feature

The matching software vendors don't employ investigators — investigation has never been their business. The BPOs employ investigators but sell labor hours, so automating investigation destroys their own revenue. Neither incumbent can follow. And every exception ReconForce investigates feeds a pattern database that makes the next investigation better — a moat that compounds daily.

04

Why might customers care?

The pain today

Thirty exceptions a day at 30–45 minutes each — more than a full working day, every day. The queue never empties, and 15–25% of analyst time disappears into the counterparty email loop alone.

What it costs

Exceptions aging past promised SLAs. Audit findings on undocumented resolutions, triggering emergency budget. A senior analyst's departure creating overnight key-person risk. Costs rising yearly with no ceiling.

Why today's coping fails

Matching software already won its battle — 96% auto-match reduced headcount by only two people. Hiring can't outpace volume growth, and 20–30% annual attrition keeps draining the pattern knowledge that lives in veterans' heads.

Why they might pay

They buy resolved exceptions at ₹200–500 each. The economics work because AI inference costs $0.05–0.50 per investigation versus $7.50–12.50 of BPO labor — a structural cost shift, not a discount. The mean investigation drops from 45 minutes of work to 5 minutes of review, measurable on day one.

02

Understand the world

Same world as ReconForce — the analyst, the queue, the counterparty. (If you haven't read that story yet, start there.) This time, zoom into one slice of the day: the email loop.

Remember the number: 15–25% of the analyst's time goes into the counterparty email loop alone. Composing an inquiry takes 10 minutes. Then comes the waiting — replies take 1 to 5 days. The reply arrives as free text plus a PDF, in whatever format the counterparty felt like using. The analyst reads it, extracts the one data point that matters, updates the exception record.

"Exceptions pending counterparty response" is the largest aging bucket in the queue. The work isn't hard — it's scattered, slow, and endless. And no tool touches it, because it all happens inside email, invisible to the matching software.
03

What would we build?

Inputs

The analyst flags an exception for counterparty follow-up. That's it — the loop is now CounterpartyClear's.

↓
Where AI helps

Drafting a contextually appropriate inquiry, monitoring for the reply, and parsing whatever comes back — free text, forwarded chains, PDFs — then extracting the resolution data.

↓
Where humans stay involved

The inquiry goes out via the analyst's own email — the counterparty sees a familiar sender, not a robot address. The analyst stays the decision-maker: they flag what needs follow-up and receive the structured summary; they never compose, chase, or interpret.

↓
Outputs

A structured summary with the extracted resolution data. Pricing per interaction: ₹100–200 (inquiry sent + reply parsed + data extracted). The "pending counterparty response" aging bucket drops toward zero.

10 minutes to compose each inquiry, 1–5 days of waiting, then manual parsing of free-text-plus-PDF replies — consuming 15–25% of every analyst's time.
