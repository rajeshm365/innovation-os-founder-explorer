EvidentFreight

An automated evidence assembler for denied freight claims — scattered records in, one dossier per claim out, built to defeat the broker's three standard denial arguments.

01

Opportunity snapshot

Category: Automated Freight Claim Evidence Assembler. Pattern: AI Vertical Software, functioning as a Multi-Agent Enterprise System — an automated back-office that pulls disparate records into structured output. Both Tier S patterns in our preference criteria.

Why it survived previous stages

Three committees, three different favorites — and one consensus. Claude favored ClockIn.ai's venture upside. Perplexity favored FreightReclaim's service-first economics. Gemini gave EvidentFreight the only GO in its review, calling it "the only company in the portfolio that perfectly balances high venture potential with the team's strict part-time, cross-border execution constraints."

The consensus saw the three survivors as a natural sequence, not rivals: EvidentFreight → FreightReclaim → ClockIn.ai. EvidentFreight is the entry point — it runs entirely on asynchronous batch data (no live integrations, no real-time clocks, no night shifts for an India-based team) and teaches us broker contracts, geofencing logic, and dispute packages, from which the later companies become reachable.

02

Understand the world

Forget startups for ten minutes. This is one day in an American trucking company — not the trucks, the office behind the trucks. Tap through, one step at a time. The company doesn't appear until the pain is obvious.

A mid-size trucking company in the US runs maybe 20 to 100 trucks. Behind those trucks sit two kinds of office people.

A dispatcher — the person who decides which truck takes which load, and who stays on the phone with drivers all day.

And a billing clerk — the person who turns finished deliveries into invoices and chases the money.

↓

When a truck delivers goods, the company gets paid for the trip. Simple. But trucking has a second kind of money, and that's where this whole story lives.

A driver arrives at a warehouse to unload. The warehouse is slow. The driver sits in his cab for four hours doing nothing. That waiting time is called detention — and the contract says the trucking company can charge for it, because a parked truck earns nothing.

There's also a middleman in most of these deals called a broker — the company that connects whoever owns the goods with whoever owns the trucks. The broker is the one the trucking company must bill, and the one who decides whether to pay.

↓

A driver texts his dispatcher: "lumper 275 walmart load" — meaning he paid $275 cash to warehouse workers to unload the Walmart shipment. The dispatcher, juggling 45 active loads at that moment, scribbles it on a sticky note. At shift end he types it into a Google Sheet. Three days later the billing clerk tries to match that note to the right load. Sometimes she gets it wrong. Sometimes it's simply lost. The $275 was real money spent — and it quietly vanishes.

Now the bigger version. That same driver waited four hours at a dock. The contract allows a detention charge — say $200–$300. The clerk files the claim with the broker.

Here's the number that should stop you: across the industry, only 29.3% of billed detention money is ever collected. More than 70% of money the trucking company has earned, on paper, never arrives.
↓

When the clerk files a claim, the broker pushes back with three standard arguments:

  • "Your driver wasn't actually there that whole time" — prove his status minute by minute.
  • "Your driver arrived late for his appointment, so the waiting is your fault."
  • "We need the signed paper documents, not your screenshots."

To answer these, the clerk needs proof from many places at once: the truck's ELD — the electronic logbook every US truck must carry, recording where the truck was and when — plus photos of delivery papers, the appointment confirmation, the contract terms, the driver's texts. These live in 4–5 different systems.

Assembling proof for ONE claim takes her 45 to 90 minutes. The evidence comes out incomplete in most cases.
↓

The clerk handles about 15 claims a week and actually collects on 4. When a claim is denied, she spends 2 hours writing a response, follows up 3–5 times, and after 60 days... gives up.

Why? Do the math she does in her head: this claim is worth $200. Fighting it costs hours of work. For claims of $100–$500, fighting costs more than the claim is worth. So the rational move is to abandon it. Every single small claim, abandoned rationally, one by one.

↓
  • The broker is structurally motivated to deny. Every denied claim is money the broker keeps. This is not rudeness — it's the permanent shape of the relationship.
  • Some contracts void claims in minutes. They say the trucking company must notify the broker within 15–30 minutes of detention starting — or the claim is void. A dispatcher watching 45 loads cannot watch 45 stopwatches.
  • The knowledge lives in one head. How each broker denies, and what works against them, lives only inside the veteran clerk's head. Billing teams have 40%+ turnover per year. When she quits, the company starts from zero.
↓

A fleet leaks $1,500–$3,000 per truck per month this way. For these companies that's 2–5% of their entire operating margin — earned, billed, and then silently written off every quarter.

The opening

The trucking company is not losing this fight because it lacks data. The ELD knows exactly where the truck sat and for how long. It loses because nobody has the time to assemble that proof into a package the broker cannot refuse — claim by claim, for claims too small to justify a human's hours.

That gap — between proof that exists and proof that gets assembled — is where the company we chose lives. Ab samajh aaya? Good — now let's see what we'd build.

03

What would we build?

EvidentFreight is an automated evidence assembler. Not live, not real-time — it works on yesterday's and last week's denied claims, in batches, on our own hours. Here's the flow, no technology jargon.

Inputs

The carrier's pile of denied claims, as ordinary historical files sent over email: PDFs, spreadsheets, ELD logs, geofence records, text logs.

No live integrations. No real-time clocks. Nothing in the carrier's infrastructure changes.

↓
What happens

For each denied claim, the scattered records are pulled together into one structured, multi-source evidence dossier — built specifically to refute the broker's three standard denial arguments: driver status, appointment fault, paper supremacy.

↓
Where AI helps

The assembly itself — the work that takes the clerk 45–90 minutes per claim across 4–5 systems. The system reads the documents, matches the records to the claim, and structures the argument. That's what makes claims too small for a human's hours suddenly worth pursuing.

↓
Where humans stay involved

The clerk still submits the package into the broker's portal — EvidentFreight owns the compilation and the argument, not the submission. And on our side, the founders run the pipelines and review outputs entirely within their own part-time hours. That's the honest shape of it: we own the evidence, not the outcome.

↓
Outputs

One dispute-ready dossier per claim. Claims that were rationally abandoned become rationally pursuable.

Why a company, not a feature

The stated moat is the accumulated cross-carrier knowledge base of broker denial patterns and winning evidentiary thresholds — something legacy systems cannot match — plus the stated expansion sequence into FreightReclaim and ClockIn.ai. Beyond that: Insufficient evidence available from previous stages.

04

Why might customers care?

The pain today

45–90 minutes to assemble evidence for one claim, across 4–5 systems — and it still comes out incomplete in most cases. Anything under $500 is rationally abandoned.

What it costs

Only 29.3% of billed detention is ever collected — 70.7% leaks. That's $1,500–$3,000 per truck per month, or 2–5% of operating margin, written off every quarter.

Why today's coping fails

The broker is structurally motivated to deny. Notification windows of 15–30 minutes void claims before anyone notices. And the knowledge of how to fight each broker lives in one veteran clerk's head, in a role with 40%+ annual turnover.

Why they might pay

This is their own written-off revenue coming back — demonstrated on their own historical denial files before they commit to anything. A carrier billing $500K/month in extra charges is leaking over $350K of it; per the locked evidence, reclaiming even 20% of that leakage yields massive savings. On pricing and contract structure specifically: Insufficient evidence available from previous stages.

FreightReclaim

Takes over the entire detention claim lifecycle — from detection through filing, dispute, and collection — charging only on recovered revenue.

01

Opportunity snapshot

Category: Freight Exception Recovery Operations. Pattern: AI-Native Service Company — the fleet's billing department never touches detention claims; FreightReclaim executes the whole lifecycle.

Why it survived — and why it's secondary, not first

Perplexity gave it a GO: strong market pain (ATRI: fewer than half of detention invoices get paid, ~$11.5B industry cost), reachable buyers, and a fast service-based validation loop — the first learning loop runs through conversations and manual delivery, not software.

But the locked sequence places it second: EvidentFreight → FreightReclaim → ClockIn.ai. EvidentFreight teaches us broker contracts, denial patterns, and dispute packages on batch data; FreightReclaim is the natural next step — adding filing, dispute management, and collection on top of the evidence engine we'll already have.

02

Understand the world

Same world as EvidentFreight — the billing clerk, the broker, the abandoned claims. (If you haven't read that story yet, start there.) The difference is how much of the fight this company takes over.

Remember the clerk's week: 15 claims handled, 4 collected. A denial means 2 hours of response writing, 3–5 follow-ups, and abandonment after 60 days. EvidentFreight hands her a dossier — but she still files it, still chases it, still decides when to give up.

FreightReclaim removes her from the fight entirely. Detection of the event, evidence assembly, filing within the contractual notification window, broker-pattern-specific dispute responses, escalation, collection — the whole lifecycle. The clerk handles exception cases only, about 5% of claims.

The pitch from the generation artifact: "You bill $180,000/year in detention. You collect $52,000. We collect $100,000. Our fee is 25% of incremental recovery. You net $36,000 more. You pay nothing if we collect nothing."
