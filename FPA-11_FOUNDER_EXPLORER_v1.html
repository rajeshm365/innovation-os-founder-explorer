01

Opportunity snapshot

Category: Financial Close Operations — a new category where the vendor owns the close, not the customer. Pattern: AI-Native Service Company — Tier S in our preference criteria: "Don't build a tool that helps people do accounting. Just do the accounting."

The locked consensus sequence: CloseFactory → MacroMigrate → PortfolioOS. Caveats that travel with this: 2-of-3 validator coverage, and the same-AI caveat (these concepts were generated and ranked by the same AI family).

02

Understand the world

Forget startups for ten minutes. Let me show you what is actually happening inside a mid-sized company group every single month. Tap through — the company doesn't appear until the pain is obvious.

There is a financial analyst sitting in a mid-sized company. Not a giant like a bank, not a small shop — a group doing roughly $50M–$500M in revenue. That group is not one company. It is many companies stitched together: maybe 10, 20, 30 separate legal companies. Each one is called an entity — just a fancy word for "one legal company inside the bigger group." One entity makes products, one handles distribution, one was bought two years ago and still runs on different software.

↓

Every month, the boss of all money matters — the CFO — must answer one question for the board: "How did the whole group do this month?"

To answer that, someone has to take the numbers from every entity and combine them into one truthful set of accounts. This combining is called consolidation. It is not simple addition. Entities sell to each other, use different account names, and submit data in different formats. Someone has to clean and merge all of it.

That someone is our analyst. And here is the key: the merging is done by a macro — a personal program he wrote inside Excel years ago, in VBA (the programming language built into Excel). Thousands of lines of code that only he understands. He built it under deadline pressure just to survive a reporting cycle, and never got the time to rebuild or document it properly.

↓

Walk through a normal month with him:

  • Days 1–5: he chases all the entities to submit their numbers. Phone calls, emails, reminders.
  • Days 5–8: he feeds everything into his macro and runs it, often overnight.
  • Days 8–10: he reviews the output, fixes oddities, adjusts.
  • Day 12 or later: the board finally sees the numbers.
That is when the macro behaves. Per the practitioner account in our validation, it breaks most months. When it breaks, only he can fix it — because only he knows what those thousands of lines do.
↓

While the macro is broken, the company is financially blind. The validated consequence: a 2–3 day reporting blackout — days where nobody can say how the group performed. If that happens during a board meeting cycle or an investor review, it is genuinely painful.

And it is worse in one specific setting: private equity (PE) firms — investment firms that buy 5–30 companies per fund. Every company they buy uses different software and different account formats. They need a combined view of the whole portfolio, and they typically hold companies only 3–7 years — too short to justify forcing everyone onto one system. So the same Excel-and-one-person pattern repeats, multiplied.

↓

The CFO knows all this. He is not ignorant. He has done the math: keeping the analyst costs about $90K a year. Replacing the macro with proper enterprise consolidation software costs $300K+ plus 6–12 months of risky, disruptive implementation. So he keeps the analyst and the macro. This is a rational, conscious decision — not negligence.

Even companies that DID buy software didn't escape: validation showed the software handles maybe most of the work, and Excel still handles the edge cases. The personal spreadsheet survives. This is called the hybrid trap.

↓
  • He gets sick. He resigns — and validation confirmed actual departures, not vague threats, are a real trigger.
  • He cannot even hand over properly, because the knowledge lives in undocumented code, not in his head as teachable steps.
  • Auditors flag the spreadsheet as a risk year after year.
  • Each new acquisition adds entities and makes the macro more fragile.
↓

Blackouts during board cycles. Audit findings. Exit and IPO preparation turning into a 6–12 month emergency cleanup. PE partners waiting weeks for portfolio numbers. And one person, trapped, holding everything up.

The opening

Here is the gap: everyone in this market sells tools — software the customer must implement and operate themselves. Nobody sells the outcome — "give us your entity data, we hand you finished consolidated accounts."

That was never economically possible before, because understanding someone's macro logic required weeks of expensive consultant time. AI can now read and reason about that VBA logic in hours, and processing all entity files costs a few dollars per run instead of hundreds. Ab samajh aaya? Good — that gap is where the company lives.

03

What would we build?

CloseFactory does the monthly consolidation FOR the customer. The analyst's consolidation role ceases to exist; he only reviews flagged exceptions. No technology jargon — just the flow.

Inputs

The customer uploads their entity data each month. Nothing gets implemented, installed, or migrated — which is exactly why the CFO's biggest objection to change disappears.

↓
What happens

A coordinated set of AI agents handles extraction, consolidation logic, and validation, with human quality review — and delivers the finished consolidated management accounts.

↓
Where AI helps

Reading and reasoning about the analyst's VBA logic in hours instead of consultant-weeks, and processing all entity files for a few dollars per run instead of hundreds. That cost collapse is what makes selling the outcome possible at all.

↓
Where humans stay involved

On the customer's side: the analyst reviews flagged exceptions only. On our side: this is a near-zero-error domain, so Ranjan's human-in-loop QA verifies output accuracy before anything is delivered, and Vikas runs onboarding, data collection, and SLA tracking. The onboarding logic-capture phase is operationally intensive — a flagged tension against our part-time hours.

↓
Outputs

Consolidated accounts on Day 2 instead of Day 12. No macro. No one-person dependency. No blackout when that person is unavailable. Pricing: ₹1.5L–₹4L/month — cheaper than the analyst (~$7.5K/month loaded) and far cheaper than enterprise software (~$25K+/month amortized).

Why a company, not a feature

It owns the entire workflow and the deliverable (Workflow Ownership grade C). It creates a new category — Financial Close Operations — where the vendor owns the close. Incumbents structurally cannot follow: their license-plus-implementation business model requires the customer to do the work. And every customer's captured consolidation logic compounds into a pattern library that powers the entire downstream ecosystem — PortfolioOS, MacroMigrate and the rest.

02

Understand the world

Same world as CloseFactory — the analyst, the macro, the rational CFO. (If you haven't read that story yet, start there.) This time, look at the choice the CFO is trapped between.

The CFO faces a binary choice: keep the fragile macro, OR implement $300K+ software with a 6–12 month risky, disruptive cutover. Both options are bad. The "known devil" beats the "unknown devil," so nothing changes — year after year.

MacroMigrate removes the choice. The macro shrinks by one component per month — each replacement small, testable, reversible. After 6–12 months, the macro is empty and all logic lives in versioned, tested AI agents. At no point did the organization "switch systems." If any piece fails, the corresponding macro component still exists.

Why this never existed before: decomposing thousands of lines of monolithic VBA into logical components required weeks of senior developer time. AI code reasoning can now find those boundaries automatically.

02

Understand the world

Same world — but climb one level up, from a single company group to the PE firm that owns many of them.

A PE firm buys 5–30 companies per fund. Every company uses different software and different account formats. The operating partner — the person responsible for making the portfolio perform — chases portfolio company CFOs for quarterly financials, an analyst manually aggregates everything in Excel, and the consolidated view arrives 3–4 weeks after quarter-end, with no entity-level comparability.

The structural trap: holding periods are 3–7 years — too short to justify forcing everyone onto one ERP system, which takes 2–3 years. So the gap between "need visibility NOW" and "standardization takes years" is permanent. PortfolioOS fills it: financial data flows in from each company in whatever format it lives in, semantic mapping normalizes it automatically ("COGS" = "Cost of Revenue" = "Direct Costs"), and a consolidated portfolio view exists within 48 hours of period-end — new acquisitions integrated in 30 days.
